Poop, Fire, and Land

July 16th 2011 Jeremiah

I survived survival school. Wew. That's good.

There's a lot of surviving that goes on at survival school actually. In fact, everyone survived.

Actually, whatever survival is, it played a pretty small role in the trip, although I'm sure everyone's experience was different. I would describe it as a wilderness expedition designed to maximize connection to, and minimize insulation from, the wilderness that one is expeditioning in.

There are three stories to tell from this experience. Okay, there are more, but there are three that speak of some powerful moments and are certainly highlights of my experience. One is about poop, one is about fire, and one is about land.

POOP: Somewhere in the middle of this 14-day course we stopped walking for a bit and setup a base camp of sorts for 2 days. During this time we slaughtered, processed, and consumed a sheep, all done in the woods with little more than our small knives and some cast iron. Part of this experience was educational: learning how to respectfully, humanely and quickly do the slaughtering, learning how to effectively clean and butcher the sheep, learning how to preserve and prepare close to all of the edible portions of the sheep. Part of this experience was experiential. A good number of us had never partaken in the slaughtering and processing of the animals that we eat, and it is certainly a powerful experience. To witness and partake in the transformation of a living animal into a delicious, nourishing, and satisfying meal was to put myself into direct contact with the flow of life from another being into myself.

The really magical moment, though, came the next morning. I had hiked up the hill for my daily morning poop. I had done my business in a small hole and as we had been taught, was mixing it into the soil to help kickstart the breaking down and fertilization process.

I froze, shivers running up and down my spine. For lack of a better way of putting this, I was experiencing myself as part of the circle of life and in a very powerful way. I felt a deep connection with the flow of life and felt myself as a part of it, or a conduit of it, or perhaps both. I had to stand there for a couple minutes just staring at my hole of poop. All I could see was the life of the sheep being transformed, yet again, into the life of the soil and the life of the land. It was a life that I had helped to take, a life that I had held onto for a little while, and a life that I was now passing back into the greater web of the living land.

The idea of the circle of life, and the flow of energy through ecological systems, is certainly something I've been familiar with for some time. But, due in a large part to the fact that in my regular life I have no direct experience of where the life that sustains me comes from and no direct experience of what, if anything, it nourishes when it leaves me, I had never had the experience of actually being a part of the circle of life or an ecological system. So, that was pretty cool.

FIRE: There are few things that define humans as much as our relationship with fire. All of modern civilization was forged on our ability to create and control flame. The transformative power of combustive heat is without equal. Without it, our tools would be rocks, our houses would be sticks, and our food would be cold.

We have so mastered the creation and control of fire that we can turn it on with the flick of a wrist or thumb, the turning of a key, or the push of a button. It is fully ingrained continuously in the fabric of our daily lives.

But what happens when we step out of our daily lives? Perhaps we step into survival school where we don't have our prefabricated (with fire) matches or lighters. Fire becomes hard and it becomes a big deal.

We had our bow drill fire lesson within the first couple days of the course. Which, if you're unfamiliar, involves a fire board to be drilled into, a spindle to do the drilling, and a bow to spin the spindle. The idea here is to create a fine sawdust by drilling the wooden spindle into the board. The sawdust falls into a notch cut into the board and when enough has accumulated the speed of the drilling is increased to heat up the sawdust and turn it into a coal. The coal can then be transferred to a tinder bundle and breathed into fire. At least that is how it's supposed to work. The actuality of the thing is a delicate balancing act of patience and perseverance because it takes some time to get everything dialed in to the level of relative flawlessness needed for the operation to succeed.

I tried unsuccessfully for the first couple nights, put it on hold during sheep camp, and then we transitioned to the solo phase of the course. For the solo phase of the course we each got our own small patch of land on which to be by ourselves for two days. We each got a portion of food (oatmeal, quinoa, lentils, a few veggies) and were left to our own devices. We started in the evening, and after getting a quick shelter setup I immediately began working my fire kit. I felt a certain amount of pressure. There wasn't a lot of light left, I didn't want to eat uncooked lentil stew, and I hand't yet successfully got a fire going. But I felt strangely confidant. I knew I had been close before, and I was feeling that this would be an extremely excellent time for a first fire.

I started afresh with a new hole in my fireboard (the fifth hole). I drilled in a little ways with my spindle and cut my notch. It was time to go at it. At first I was having trouble creating sawdust. I added some pine sap as lubrication between the spindle and my top rock and also added a bit of sand into the hole I was drilling into the board. The goal was to force as much friction to the end of the spindle that was drilling into the board as possible, while reducing the friction at the top of the spindle. It worked. I started getting a lot of sawdust, sped up my strokes and the sawdust started smoking. I had a coal! I carefully transferred it to my pre-made tinder bundle and started giving it long slow breaths. After five or six of these there was nothing left glowing in my tinder bundle. I'd lost the coal.

Evening was coming on, and the pressure was building. Could I get this fire lit before dark? I still felt confidant and knew I needed to stay calm if I was going to succeed. I added some powdered and fibrous oak leaves to my tinder bundle and some crushed up, dried out pine needles. I used the same hole on my fire board and the coal came quickly. I did another careful transfer to my tinder bundle and again started long slow breaths. This time I could see the coal growing and passing on its glowing life to the other elements of the tinder bundle. And it was starting to smoke, a lot, a good sign the fire was on its way. I continued the long slow breaths and the smoke started pouring into my eyes. I couldn't see and my eyes were burning, but I had to continue. A long deep and powerful breath and I felt that my eyes were now actually burning instead of just stinging from smoke. I moved the tinder bundle away from my face and squinted open my eyes. I was holding fire in my hand! The tiny flames were leaping around my tinder bundle quickly getting bigger. I transferred the flaming mass into my fire pit and quickly nursed the tinder into a small but powerful campfire.

I was ecstatic. And I was in disbelief. Partially at what I had accomplished, but also at the effect it was having on me. I started this story talking about how fire permeates modern human life. I can say now that not only does it permeate our life, but it permeates our genes as well. Conjuring the spirit of fire from the raw landscape struck an unexpectedly primal and deep seated chord within me. I sat down, cooked my lentils, and gazed, mesmorized, into my fire, basking in the radiance of the power I had unleashed.

[As a side note, if you ever find yourself breathing a tinder bundle to life, hold the thing above the level of your face so as to avoid the discomfort of smoke and flames in the eyes. :-)]

LAND: This section may also be titled place, or presence, or belonging, or harmonious existing, or any number of other similar names, but it was the land that tied it all together, and it was the land that was there for me to step into, and it was the land that I became a part of, so this section is titled land.

It was the last day of hiking, the day before we all began making our way to our respective homes. It is hard for me to know what brought on this experience. It could have been the days of constant hiking, it could have been the distance I felt from the rat race, it could have been the constant living in closer connection with the means of my existence, but what it felt like is that I said, "I am here, now", and the land said "Welcome. You belong here, now."

To describe this experience more is going to be difficult, but it was dominated by two things. First, the constant monologue that defines my life ceased. That little voice, the constant narrator and analyzer of existence finally shut his trap, and in his place, a quiet stillness welled up inside of me. This experience, of being in the flow, or being the moment, is one that I've had at various times before. Usually it will happen in an intense high focus activity (like skiing) but sometimes it will also happen during quieter times as well. It basically amounts to my minds acceptance to just be in whatever moment it finds itself and not go willy-nilly thinking about other moments, or worse, getting stuck in the meta-moment of moment analyzation.

This time, however, was a bit different in that there was a second factor. Namely, it seemed like it was not just my own mind that was content and stoked to experience itself in the exact moment it was in but something else as well welcomed me into the moment and corroborated my existence in that place at that time. The feeling I got was that it was the place, the land, the desert, that accepted me into it, in effect saying that I was a part of that land as well.

This is what I've been working towards. This is what my expeditions into the wilderness are for. The feeling that not only do I belong on and in the land, but that I am integrated with the workings of the land, and that my life is a piece of the greater whole of the living earth.

This feeling lasted with me for most of the rest of the day. And to diminishing degrees, it stays with me still. A small, but constant reminder that life is everywhere, and everywhere it is connected.

I'm a current student of the Wilderness Awareness School Anake program class of 2015.
