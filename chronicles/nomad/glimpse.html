Glimpse

September 26th 2010 Jeremiah

Getting off a bus at the end of a long ride is always a surreal experience. Time ceases to exist while on a bus that travels through the day and night. You can't figure out when to sleep and you can't sleep when you want. Eventually you can't remember how long its been since you started, or where you started. The outside world becomes a shadowy blur passing by through tinted windows. And the fact that you once walked that world becomes a distant memory, or a dream. The upshot of this is when I got off the bus a little over a week ago in Iron River, Michigan at 5AM in the "morning" I had little idea where I was, what I was doing, or even if I was awake or not.

Iron River, Michigan is pretty dark at 5AM and very deserted. Its a small enough town that streetlights are few and far between (and flickering) and gas stations are not open 24 hours. It's a town towards the far end of bus lines, nestled on the far western side of the Upper Peninsula of Michigan right next to Wisconsin. I'd been the only one on the bus for the last 4 hours or so, and according to the bus driver, that wasn't abnormal at all for this part of the country. The word remote was invented in the next town over. Now if your asking what in tarnation I was doing there you're asking the same question I was asking myself in the wee hours of that morning. But before we can understand that we're going to have to look at a little bit of (my) history.

Two epiphanies have occurred in the last two years of my life the aftermath of which are largely responsible for my current direction. The first happened about 2 years ago. Here is an excerpt from my journal from October 20th, 2008 which happened to be a monday (maybe my whole life philosophy is now just a hooped up case of the mondays.. :-):

"And woa! I'm also just now considering some major life changes: sell off all my extraneous shit. Sell the iMac. Sell off my DPS skis. Keep only one pair. Sell off anything else that I don't need, give away all my junk. Streamline my life. I'm currently living in full realization that my current life is not ideal - why should I keep fucking around? Do I have the will power to do this? Because I may not find my true path until I get rid of the maze that is my current life. Just do it."

If you can't read it in there, I was pretty surprised to find my self writing those words. And while I didn't immediately act on those thoughts the seed was planted and a year later I found myself amazingly leaving Utah jobless and homeless with the sum total of my belongings on my back and approximately no idea where I was going or what I was doing. Which leads me to epiphany number two which happened shortly after I left Utah. This epiphany happened to me on a beautiful rocky river while I was taking a fitness course in West Virginia and is mentioned in my journal with only the following line:

"Knowing the woods are for me and I am for the woods."

This time the thought didn't seem like a surprise so much as a realization of a fact I had known but forgotten for quite some time. Even still the results of the thought were not immediately apparent and are in fact still culminating. The thought, however, certainly began a questioning and exploration of the natural realm and my relationship to it. Since I was a child I've been drawn to the woods but this time the draw seemed different. The more I questioned and explored in this direction the more I felt compelled to experience the wilderness and a real relationship with it. But the more I experienced the wilderness the more frustrated I became with my inability to enter into the relationship with it that I felt was innate to the wilderness itself - one of harmony with itself. I was constantly getting poison ivy or being driven away by mosquitos or injury. I felt like I was bringing my old life patterns and needs with me into the woods and in so doing finding myself unable to adopt the patterns and habits of the woods itself. But I was certainly not deterred in my quest and on such a quest sooner or later one is bound to end up in Iron River, Michigan.

As I got off the bus, gathered my bags, and stepped into the dark parking lot, I looked around, trying to get myself oriented. Down at the end of the parking lot was a lone station wagon barely visible in the shadows. I walked toward it with my fingers crossed. Soon I was able to make out "Teaching Drum Outdoor School" written on the side in large balloon-like letters. A young women was getting out of the car as I pulled up. We introduced ourselves, determining I was in the right place, and soon my bags were in the car and we were passing quickly out of the small town and into the woods as the first rays of dawn streaked across the clouds.

I still felt like I was in a dream after the bus ride and the forrest around us still seem surreal. But as the dawn broke down the shadows the experience felt more and more real. We drove on empty roads through the woods of Michigan and passed without fanfare into the woods of northern Wisconsin. The land here was mostly flat, but rolled just enough to create a great patchwork of forests and bogs and a great maze of lakes and creeks.

When we arrived at the school it was still quite early in the morning. The sun was hidden by mostly cloudy skies but it had still risen only recently. We pulled up the short driveway into the midst of a small collection of wooden buildings that constituted the support center for the school. This was a collection of administration buildings and homes for the small community of people that supported the school. This group of 10-15 people were standing in a small circle on a patch of grass as we drove in and began to disperse for the days activities as I was getting myself out of the car and taking in the new surroundings.

I quickly met Matt, a soft-spoken and smiling guy with long hair and a a full woodsmans beard, who had been my main contact at the school so far and had been in charge of organizing my visit to the school. He gave me a quick tour of the support center, went over my gear and set me up with a new knife and a set of wool long johns. After a quick nap I was back in the station wagon with a small one-person canoe tied up top and Matt was driving me in search of the school's seekers -- their way of talking about their students. I had come to this school to visit for a week the current participants of the school's Wilderness Guide Program - an 11-month wilderness immersion and living experience, and to see for myself if returning to the school next year for the full program was something I wanted to do.

Matt dropped me off at a boat launch on a large creek where I met 3 of the current year's seekers who had come to pick up food dropped off by the support center. These 3 seemed a bit worn out at first impression and wet and not entirely warm on this chill morning, but they were in good spirits and quite friendly. Introductions were quick as there seemed to be quite the interest to return to camp. The seekers live as a group in the woods, removed as much as possible for the year from the trappings of modern civilization. They are learning to sustain the life of their community with what the earth provides in the forest that surrounds them. The school considers its role to be one of support and indirect guidance to the seekers and as such takes a hands off approach as much as possible. The school's instructors - guides - don't live with the seekers but visit frequently to help keep the experience on track.

After determining how to load myself and my backpacks worth of gear into the small and very unstable canoe, the four of us were off paddling up the slowly moving creek. It was slightly chilly and the mornings off-and-on-rain returned with a drizzle. I felt incredibly excited to be there and the rain didn't dampen my spirits in the slightest. I was finally able to feel myself relax after the hectic travels. After a short while of paddling into the wind and the rain the drizzle passed and soon we were hauling the canoes up on an unmarked shore, hiding them in the trees and bushwhacking our way a bit farther up the creek to the seekers current encampment.

I had learned earlier at the support center that the seekers were not at their normal camp but were currently at a temporary camp set up to gather swamp grass for the thatching of a wigwam they were constructing. They had just arrived at this new camp a day or two before I joined them and had setup a loose collection of small tarp shelters and lean-tos to house them for their time there. With the break in the rain I setup my tent as quickly as possible then joined a small group of the seekers around their fire as they divided up the food that had just been picked up and started cooking a bit for lunch. It seemed that most were waiting out the weather in their shelters and soon it started raining again and I was in my tent taking my second much need nap of the day.

I awoke late in the day to no rain and a bit of sun and joined in with wood gathering for the evening fire. I met the rest of the seekers slowly as they appeared mysteriously out of their tarps or the trees. There were 7 in total. 3 Americans, 2 from Germany, 1 from France and 1 from Ireland. Ages ranging from 19 to mid 40's. 3 women and 4 men. Quite an interesting and diverse group. I settled in to watch and experience what it was that had drawn such different people to the same little spot in the woods and what they were doing there together.

Over the next 7 days I experienced a lot and learned even more. Learning to make a life in the wilderness seems to be quite the transition. Going into the visit I was thinking a year seemed like a long time to spend in the woods. Coming out it is apparent it would merely be the first small step on a journey. It was a rough week for the seekers. They were separated these seven days from the home they had been building in the woods these past few months and faced with the daunting prospect of gathering tremendous amounts of swamp grass from the swamp that bordered our creek. They were at the tail end of the summer hunger months so food was scarce. Tension and peace was washing over the group like the tide. They were, as they told it, battling with the fourth threshold of the wilderness experience - the relinquishing of personal ego in favor of circle consciousness. One seeker found it too much at the time and moved on while I was there. I helped out with the physical activities as much as I could but found myself able only to watch the emotional dynamics play themselves out. The seekers were all incredible, I can only hope that if I do this next year, the next batch will be as cool is this one. When the time came for me to leave, my feet were tired, my hands were scarred and bleeding, and my heart was happy.

The school's motto reads

where wilderness is the classroom ancient voices are the teachers knowing self and balance are the quest

Knowing the wilderness here is as much a means as it is an end. The scope of what they are doing is much broader than "learning survival skills" or even "learning how to live in balance with the wild." The quest for self and its harmonious co-existence with and in the the stormy billows of the world is one with which I am familiar. But to pursue it outside the reach of insidiously theoretical dusty old books and inside the experience of the real earth and surrounded by relations of all types (humans, birds, chipmunks, trees, rocks and rivers) that are also of the same pursuit seems a bit too much like a dream come true.

Which makes me wonder, now that I'm back in the world of glass and concrete and electric lights, if the whole week was just a glimpse through the window of a cross country bus by a sleep deprived mind of what my life might look like, someday.

I'm a current student of the Wilderness Awareness School Anake program class of 2015.
